Feynman Diagrams

In this framework, Feynman diagrams are interpreted as compositional graphs of allowed transitions. Vertices correspond to irreducible or effective transitions; internal lines correspond to aggregations that mediate composition without registering as interface states; and external lines represent stabilized transition patterns.

This interpretation aligns with the operational role of Feynman diagrams in quantum field theory while explicitly avoiding particle reification.

In a simple Feynman diagram, an electron \((e^-)\) and a positron \((e^+)\) annihilate (transition), producing a photon \((\gamma)\) that later becomes (transition) a quark–antiquark pair. In our formulation, Feynman diagrams are not merely “handy” visual tools, but structurally natural objects.

Guiding intuition:
> Transitions correspond to operations or processes, typically expressed by verbs in human language.

We now make this precise and show why Feynman diagrams fit our framework almost perfectly.

1. Why Feynman diagrams are natural in our ontology

In standard QFT language, Feynman diagrams are often misread as literal pictures of particles moving in spacetime. Operationally, however, they function as:

  • Bookkeeping devices for allowed transitions between interaction states

This matches our axioms.

In our formulation:

  • Transitions are operations
  • States are interfaces
  • Particles are stabilized patterns of transitions

Thus, a Feynman diagram is best understood as a compositional graph of allowed transitions, subject to causal and symmetry constraints.

2. Recasting the example in our functional language

Standard QFT description

An electron and a positron annihilate, producing a photon, which later produces a quark–antiquark pair.

Functional formulation

Let:

  • \((f_n)\) be an interface state containing an \((e^-)\) and \((e^+)\) pattern
  • \((d\tau_1)\) be the annihilation transition
  • \((d\tau_2)\) be the pair-production transition

Then:

\[ f_{n+1} = \mathcal{C}(d\tau_1) \]

\[ f_{n+2} = \mathcal{C}(d\tau_2) \]

More fundamentally, the diagram represents the composition:

\[ d\tau_2 \circ d\tau_1 \]

This is a composition of transitions, not objects moving through spacetime.

The photon \((\gamma)\) is not a thing; it is an intermediate, composable transition pattern.
This is exactly how virtual particles function operationally.

3. Virtual particles as non-recomposed aggregations

This is where the aggregation concept fits cleanly. In our ontology, the internal photon line is:

  • Not a successor state
  • Not a registered interface
  • A parallel aggregation that successfully recomposes

Thus:

A virtual particle is a composable aggregation of irreducible transitions that mediates between two effective transitions without becoming an interface state.

That single sentence fully reinterprets virtual particles in our language.

4. Feynman diagrams as computational graphs

Each Feynman diagram is:

  • A directed acyclic graph (DAG)
  • Vertices = allowed transitions
  • Edges = composability constraints
  • Weights = amplitudes derived from aggregation statistics

Therefore, Feynman diagrams are native objects in our computational universe; graphical representations of allowed compositions. We are not using Feynman diagrams metaphorically; our ontology almost requires them..

5. Why “transitions are verbs” is technically precise

In our framework:

  • Nouns (particles) correspond to stabilized, self-reinforcing patterns of transitions
  • Verbs (transitions) are the ontological primitives
  • Grammar (symmetries, conservation laws) defines the rules of composability

This mirrors the structure of interaction Lagrangians:

  • They constrain which transitions may compose
  • Rather than postulating which objects “exist”

Thus, when we say “transitions are verbs”, we are making a precise technical claim:

The fundamental ontology is morphism-first, not object-first.

This stance aligns naturally with category-theoretic and process-based formalisms.

6. What this framework buys us

  1. No particle mysticism
    No confusion about what a “virtual particle” really is.

  2. Natural handling of off-shell processes
    Aggregations that do not register as interfaces are allowed.

  3. Computational clarity
    Diagrams are executable composition graphs.

  4. Scale independence
    The same language applies from Planck-scale physics to cosmology.
